<p class="p1">Daily, I see QA articles suggested to me on LinkedIn. Some articles are from professionals I don’t even follow or connect to. I saw a similar pattern in these articles.</p>
<p class="p1">I wanted to identify it by reaching out to QAs and asking questions. Have a small discussion with random Engineers about how they feel about their profession, but it was very difficult as many deny it.<span class="Apple-converted-space"> </span></p>
<p class="p1">I wanted to do an analysis backed by data. So I gathered data. I collected LinkedIn posts. Only the post. With some comments. Not the author.<span class="Apple-converted-space">  </span>So far, it’s 35 LinkedIn posts. I know it’s not a big number to get a judgment. But let’s analyse this now.<span class="Apple-converted-space">  </span>Let’s try to understand what the thread is here. What is the signal QAs are expressing?<span class="Apple-converted-space"> </span></p>
<p class="p1">This is an unbiased analysis using Claude and ChatGPT. This is a very objective analysis; everyone should be aware.</p>

AI is simultaneously a threat and an opportunity. What the dataset tells us most clearly is that the profession is not waiting to be told what it will be. QA professionals still see themselves as essential, but fewer believe that traditional late-stage testing alone is enough. The dominant mindset is that quality work must move earlier, become more technical, and increasingly incorporate AI. At the same time, the posts reveal unease: hiring is tighter, expectations are rising, manual-only paths look fragile, and there is growing concern that teams may either marginalize QA or misuse AI without sufficient controls. The net picture is a community that is alert, ambitious, and trying to redefine its value around engineering depth, risk judgment, and business impact before the market defines it for them.</p>
